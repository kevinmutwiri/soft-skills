# Product Requirements Document

> [home](./README.md)

A PRD outlines the specifications and requirements of your solution. It is a guide for the engineering and design teams who are building out the solution: what the solution is, how it works, and what it should achieve.

The document describes the product’s purpose, functionality, user requirements, constraints, and any other relevant information that helps shape the product’s development.

A PRD also helps you understand your constraints, and how you can overcome them. It gives you an edge when a “potential investor” questions your solution’s feasibility!

Finally, a PRD allows your team to track progress and ensure that the final solution meets your specified requirements.

## How to Create a PRD

Define Goals and Objectives

Establish the goals and objectives you want to achieve with your product. What specific outcomes do you hope to accomplish? Setting clear goals helps guide your decision-making process and keeps the project focused.

Conduct User Research

To create a successful product, it’s crucial to understand the needs and preferences of your target users. Conduct user research by interviewing potential users, creating surveys, or analyzing existing data. This will help you gather insights into user expectations, pain points, and desired features.

Document User Stories

As you may remember, user stories are narratives that describe specific user interactions with your product. They help capture the desired functionality from the user’s perspective. Write down user stories that represent different use cases and scenarios, detailing the actions the user takes and the expected outcomes.

Specify Functional and Non-Functional Requirements

Functional requirements define the features and capabilities your product should have. These can include actions, inputs, outputs, and other functionalities. Non-functional requirements, on the other hand, cover aspects such as performance, security, scalability, or usability. Document both types of requirements in your PRD to ensure a comprehensive overview of what needs to be developed.

Include Design Considerations

Depending on your product, design considerations may play a crucial role. Consider how the product should look, feel, and behave from a user interface (UI) and user experience (UX) perspective. Include design guidelines, wireframes, or mockups in your PRD to communicate the desired visual and interactive aspects.

Review and Iterate

Creating a PRD is an iterative process, meaning you regularly review it and do it again. Share your document with stakeholders and team members, seeking their feedback and input. Incorporate their suggestions and make necessary revisions to improve the clarity and completeness of your PRD.

Remember, creating a PRD is about effectively communicating your vision for the product and ensuring that everyone involved understands the goals and requirements.

### Identifying Constraints

It is also common in the product development process that you identify constraints, potential limits, or problematic aspects of your solution.

To identify constraints in your solution, consider the following questions:

Thinking about what you’ve learned in Month 1 about ethical considerations, does your solution have any ethical concerns? What are those concerns?

What are some strategies you can take to mitigate ethical concerns?

What are some constraints or dependencies (a condition or item the product will rely on) you should account for?

What are some strategies you can take to address these constraints or dependencies?

What are your assumptions about your product and your target product users?

[This video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sIDxlkQ5g3A) explains what constraints are and how they’re different from goals and criteria. As you watch this video, think about:

- What makes something a constraint?
- How is a constraint different from a criterion?
- What are some examples of constraints?
